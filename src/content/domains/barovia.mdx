Barovia is a narrow valley sealed off from the outside world by a permanent wall
of mist. It is the oldest and best known of the cursed prison-realms, and the one
against which every other is measured. Its ruler is a vampire whose personal
tragedy has become the whole land's condition: the valley is perpetually
overcast, its people are watchful and fatalistic, and hope inside its borders is
a fragile, mostly private thing.

## Geography and mood

The valley floor holds a scattering of settlements, forests of dark pine, a
still lake, and a river that winds beneath heavy cloud. Above it all stands the
lord's castle, perched on a crag so that its silhouette is visible from much of
the land below — a constant reminder of who rules here. Days are grey, nights are
long, and the weather rarely lifts. Wolves and worse move through the treeline
after dark, and the roads between towns feel far longer than their distances
suggest.

## The Mists

The fog that surrounds Barovia is not ordinary weather. It functions as a border
and a cage at once: it draws in travellers from other worlds who lose their way,
and it refuses to release those the land's master wishes to keep. The Mists make
the valley a closed system, a stage on which the same griefs play out again and
again for whoever happens to be caught inside.

## Life under the count

The people of the valley live under an old, watchful tyranny. They keep to their
homes after dark, mistrust strangers, and speak of their ruler in careful,
lowered voices. Faith persists but is strained; superstition and hard practicality
guide daily life more than any promise of rescue. Newcomers are often treated as
either doomed or dangerous — sometimes both.

<SpoilerWrapper level="dm-only" summary="The nature of the curse">

The valley's misery is not incidental — it is bound to its ruler's own
punishment. He is trapped with exactly the thing he most wants and can never
truly have, and the land echoes that frustration back at everyone within it. A
recurring figure who resembles a woman from his past appears across the
generations, drawing him into the same doomed pursuit each time, which is the
engine that keeps the tragedy turning. Running the valley well means playing that
cycle as inevitability closing in, not as a fight the newcomers were ever
expected to win cleanly.

</SpoilerWrapper>
